Overview

What Are Debt Funds & Why They Matter

Debt funds (also known as private credit platforms or alternative lenders) are non-bank lending institutions that originate commercial real estate loans using capital raised from institutional investors — pension funds, sovereign wealth funds, endowments, family offices, and insurance companies. They emerged as a major force in commercial lending after the 2008 financial crisis, when bank regulations tightened and traditional lenders pulled back from transitional and higher-leverage lending.

Today, debt funds collectively manage hundreds of billions in commercial real estate lending capital and are responsible for a significant share of all bridge, transitional, and construction lending in the United States. Their growth reflects a fundamental structural shift in the capital markets: banks are increasingly constrained by regulation, while institutional investors are attracted to the yield premiums offered by commercial real estate debt.

For borrowers, debt funds offer three critical advantages over traditional bank lending: speed (closings in 2–3 weeks vs. 45–90 days for banks), flexibility (higher leverage, creative structures, willingness to finance transitional assets), and certainty of execution (fewer regulatory hurdles, faster decision-making, and larger individual loan capacities).

Why Borrowers Choose Debt Funds Over Banks

The decision between a debt fund and a bank often comes down to three factors: speed, leverage, and the ability to finance a business plan that a bank's credit committee will not approve. Debt funds excel in all three areas because they are not subject to the same regulatory constraints (Dodd-Frank, Basel III, HVCRE classifications) that limit bank lending.

Speed to Close

  • Debt funds: 2–3 weeks typical, sub-10 days possible
  • Banks: 45–90 days typical
  • Debt fund decisions made by investment committee (3–5 people), not a multi-layered bank credit approval process
  • Critical advantage in competitive acquisition scenarios where sellers demand fast, certain closings

Flexibility & Leverage

  • Leverage: Up to 80% LTV / 85% LTC (vs. 65–70% for banks)
  • Future funding: Debt funds fund renovation/capex budgets that banks typically will not
  • Structure: Interest-only, flexible prepayment, extension options
  • Property types: Debt funds finance assets that banks decline (hospitality, transitional, construction)

Debt Fund vs. Bank Bridge vs. Hard Money

Understanding the differences between these three short-term capital sources is critical for selecting the right financing for your business plan.

Debt Funds

  • Rates: SOFR + 275–500 bps
  • Leverage: Up to 80% LTV / 85% LTC
  • Closing: 2–3 weeks
  • Origination Fee: 0.50%–1.50%
  • Loan Size: $3M–$200M+
  • Funded Capex: Yes, through future advances
  • Non-Recourse: Available above $10M
  • Best for: Institutional-quality bridge, value-add, construction

Bank Bridge & Hard Money

  • Bank Bridge Rates: SOFR + 200–350 bps
  • Bank Leverage: Up to 65–70% LTV
  • Bank Closing: 45–90 days
  • Bank Best for: Lower-risk bridge with existing relationship
  • Hard Money Rates: 10%–14%+
  • Hard Money Leverage: 60%–70% LTV
  • Hard Money Closing: 1–2 weeks
  • Hard Money Best for: Last-resort, time-critical, small balance

FAQ

Debt Fund Loans FAQ

A debt fund (also called private credit or alternative lender) is a non-bank lending platform that originates commercial real estate loans using capital raised from institutional investors such as pension funds, endowments, family offices, and insurance companies. Debt funds fill the gap between traditional bank lending and hard money, offering higher leverage and more flexibility than banks with more competitive pricing than hard money lenders.
Debt funds can close in as little as 2 to 3 weeks for straightforward transactions, compared to 45–90 days for banks or CMBS. This speed advantage is critical for borrowers competing in acquisitions, facing loan maturities, or executing time-sensitive business plans. Some debt funds can even close in under 10 business days for repeat borrowers or pre-approved deals.
Debt fund rates are typically quoted as a spread over SOFR (Secured Overnight Financing Rate). Current pricing ranges from SOFR + 275 bps for low-leverage senior loans on stabilized assets to SOFR + 500+ bps for higher-leverage, higher-risk transitional deals. With SOFR around 4.30% in early 2026, all-in rates generally range from 7.05% to 9.30%+. Rates depend on leverage, property type, market, sponsor experience, and business plan complexity.
Debt funds and hard money lenders are both non-bank sources, but they differ significantly in cost, leverage, and sophistication. Debt funds are institutional platforms (typically $500M+ AUM) that price competitively (SOFR + 275–500) and offer higher leverage (up to 80% LTV). Hard money lenders are smaller, often individual-funded operations that charge higher rates (10–14%+), lower leverage (60–70% LTV), and higher fees (2–4 points). Debt funds are appropriate for institutional-quality transactions; hard money is typically a last resort for deals that cannot access institutional capital.
Debt funds are the most flexible capital source in commercial real estate and will consider virtually any property type, including multifamily, industrial, office, retail, hospitality, self-storage, mixed-use, manufactured housing, student housing, medical office, and even ground-up construction. Their willingness to finance transitional assets, value-add plays, and non-stabilized properties sets them apart from banks, life companies, and CMBS, which generally require stabilized cash flow.
