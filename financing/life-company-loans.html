Overview

Why Life Companies Offer the Best Rates in Market

Life insurance companies invest billions of dollars annually into commercial real estate mortgages as part of their general account investment portfolios. Their source of capital is unique: they are investing the premium reserves collected from policyholders, which represent long-dated liabilities that must be matched with long-dated, stable-return assets. Commercial real estate mortgages are an ideal match for this liability structure.

Unlike banks (which pay depositors for their capital) or CMBS lenders (which must compensate bond investors), life insurance companies have the lowest cost of capital in the commercial lending ecosystem. This structural advantage flows directly to borrowers in the form of tighter spreads and more competitive all-in rates. On a typical deal, a life company will price 25 to 75 basis points below what a CMBS conduit would offer for the same property — a difference that compounds into significant savings over a 10- to 25-year loan term.

Life companies also tend to be relationship-oriented, long-term holders of their mortgage positions. They do not securitize or sell their loans. This means borrowers deal with a single counterparty throughout the loan term, and the lender has an inherent interest in maintaining a constructive relationship. Modifications, assumptions, and other servicing matters are handled by the originating institution rather than a third-party special servicer.

Our Access

Why Most Brokers Cannot Access Life Companies

Life insurance companies operate very differently from banks and CMBS conduits. Most life companies do not accept unsolicited loan submissions. Instead, they work exclusively through approved correspondent relationships — typically large national brokerage platforms and a handful of established regional correspondents who have been vetted and approved by the life company's investment committee.

This correspondent model serves as a quality filter. Life companies receive a manageable volume of pre-screened, well-structured deals from brokers who understand their credit boxes, rather than hundreds of unqualified submissions. For borrowers, this means that accessing life company capital requires a broker with the right relationships and institutional credibility.

The Process

Life Company Underwriting: What to Expect

Life insurance company underwriting is thorough but efficient. Unlike CMBS, which relies on standardized metrics and rating agency frameworks, life companies make portfolio lending decisions based on a holistic assessment of property quality, market fundamentals, tenant creditworthiness, and sponsor experience. The emphasis is on long-term stability and predictability of cash flows.

Key Underwriting Criteria

  • Property quality: Institutional-grade construction, well-maintained, modern systems
  • Market fundamentals: Strong MSA demographics, employment growth, limited new supply
  • Tenant quality: Creditworthy tenants, diversified rent roll, long-term leases
  • Occupancy: Minimum 85%+ (typically 90%+ preferred)
  • Sponsor experience: Track record of successful ownership and management
  • DSCR: Minimum 1.25x–1.50x debt service coverage

Timeline (45–75 Days)

  • Week 1–2: Application, preliminary indication, site inspection
  • Week 2–3: Formal application and commitment letter issued
  • Week 3–5: Third-party reports (appraisal, environmental, PCA)
  • Week 4–6: Underwriting and investment committee approval
  • Week 5–8: Loan document preparation and closing
  • Rate lock: Available at application or commitment (varies by company)

Comparison

Life Company vs. CMBS vs. Bank: When to Use Each

Choosing between a life company, CMBS conduit, and bank depends on the borrower's priorities. No single capital source is universally best — the optimal choice depends on the specific deal characteristics and borrower objectives.

Choose Life Company When:

  • Best rate is the top priority
  • Leverage need is moderate (55–65% LTV)
  • Long-term fixed rate desired (10–25 years)
  • Property is institutional quality in a strong market
  • Borrower values relationship-oriented servicing
  • Flexible prepayment options preferred

Choose CMBS or Bank When:

  • CMBS: Higher leverage needed (65–75% LTV)
  • CMBS: Property is stabilized but not institutional-grade
  • CMBS: Borrower wants assumable, non-recourse at scale
  • Bank: Speed is critical (30–45 day close)
  • Bank: Flexibility on prepayment needed
  • Bank: Borrower has strong deposit/banking relationship

Life Company Loans FAQ

A life insurance company (life co) loan is a commercial mortgage originated by an insurance company's investment arm. Life companies invest their policyholders' premium reserves into long-term, stable assets — including commercial real estate mortgages. Because they match long-dated liabilities (insurance policies) with long-dated assets (mortgages), life companies can offer the longest fixed-rate terms (up to 25+ years) and the most competitive rates in the commercial mortgage market.
Life insurance companies have the lowest cost of capital in commercial real estate lending because they invest policyholders' reserves rather than borrowed money. They don't need to pay depositors (like banks) or bond investors (like CMBS). Combined with their preference for lower-leverage, high-quality assets, this results in spreads that are typically 25–75 basis points tighter than CMBS for comparable deals.
Most life insurance companies only accept loan submissions through approved correspondent relationships — typically large national brokerage platforms and a select number of regional correspondents. They do not accept unsolicited submissions from individual borrowers or smaller mortgage brokers. Commercial Lending Solutions has direct access to life company capital through our CBRE/MMCC pedigree and established correspondent relationships.
Life companies prefer stabilized, institutional-quality assets in strong markets. Ideal property types include Class A and B+ multifamily apartments, industrial and logistics facilities, grocery-anchored retail centers, medical office buildings, and net lease properties with credit tenants. The property should have stable occupancy (typically 85%+), a proven operating history, and be located in a primary or strong secondary market.
Life insurance companies typically lend at 55% to 65% loan-to-value (LTV), which is lower than CMBS (up to 75%) or agency (up to 80%). The trade-off for lower leverage is significantly better pricing, longer fixed-rate terms, more flexible prepayment options, and a smoother borrower experience. For borrowers who don't need maximum proceeds, life company financing is almost always the optimal choice.
