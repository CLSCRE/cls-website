CMBS Loans for Commercial Real Estate

Commercial Mortgage-Backed Securities (CMBS) loans provide non-recourse, fixed-rate financing for stabilized commercial properties starting at $5M and scaling well above $100M. By tapping the capital markets rather than a single bank's balance sheet, CMBS offers borrowers competitive long-term rates, higher leverage, and standardized execution — making it one of the most important capital sources in commercial real estate. Overview

How CMBS Lending Works

CMBS is a form of securitized lending where a commercial mortgage is originated by a conduit lender (typically a large investment bank or commercial bank), pooled with other commercial mortgages, and sold to investors as rated bonds in the capital markets. The borrower interacts with the originating lender during underwriting and closing, but after securitization the loan is managed by a special servicer.

Because the capital ultimately comes from bond investors rather than a bank's balance sheet, CMBS lenders are not constrained by the same concentration limits and regulatory capital requirements that restrict bank lending. This allows CMBS to offer higher leverage (up to 75% LTV), longer fixed-rate terms (up to 10 years), and true non-recourse structures that most banks cannot match.

The trade-off is standardization. CMBS loans follow structured underwriting guidelines, require third-party reports (appraisal, environmental, property condition), and have rigid prepayment provisions (defeasance or yield maintenance). Borrowers who value flexibility in prepayment or ongoing property management may prefer bank or life company financing. But for borrowers seeking maximum proceeds, competitive fixed rates, and non-recourse protection, CMBS is often the optimal solution.

Conduit CMBS

  • Loan sizes: $5M – $50M (typical)
  • Pooled with 50–100+ loans per securitization
  • Standardized underwriting and documentation
  • 5, 7, or 10-year fixed-rate terms
  • Non-recourse with standard carve-outs
  • Best for: mid-size stabilized assets

SASB (Single-Asset/Single-Borrower)

  • Loan sizes: $75M+ (typically $100M+)
  • Single loan securitized individually
  • Customized terms and structure
  • Floating or fixed-rate options
  • More flexibility on prepayment and reserves
  • Best for: large, institutional-quality assets
Who It's For

Ideal Borrower & Property Profile

CMBS financing is designed for stabilized, cash-flowing commercial properties with predictable income streams. The underwriting focuses primarily on the property's in-place net operating income and market fundamentals rather than the borrower's personal financial strength — though all CMBS loans require a creditworthy guarantor for standard non-recourse carve-outs.

The strongest CMBS candidates are properties with occupancy above 85%, diversified tenant rosters, strong market locations, and a demonstrated operating history. Property types that perform well in the CMBS market include multifamily, industrial, anchored retail, select-service hospitality, self-storage, and stabilized office. Single-tenant, short-term lease, and specialty properties can be more challenging but are still financeable through the right conduit.

The Process

The CMBS Loan Process: Application to Closing

Understanding the CMBS timeline and process is critical for borrowers accustomed to bank lending. CMBS transactions follow a structured path from initial application through securitization, with key milestones that differ significantly from portfolio lending.

CMBS Timeline (60–90 Days)

  • Week 1–2: Application, preliminary sizing, term sheet issued
  • Week 2–4: Third-party reports ordered (appraisal, environmental Phase I, property condition report, seismic if applicable)
  • Week 3–5: Underwriting — rent rolls, financials, lease abstracts reviewed
  • Week 5–7: Credit committee approval, final sizing confirmed
  • Week 6–8: Loan documents drafted and negotiated
  • Week 8–12: Closing, funding, and securitization

Key Documents Required

  • Trailing 12-month operating statements (T-12)
  • Current rent roll with lease expirations
  • Three years of historical financial statements
  • Property tax bills and insurance certificates
  • Capital expenditure history and budget
  • Borrower organizational documents and net worth statement
Prepayment

Defeasance vs. Yield Maintenance

CMBS loans do not allow free prepayment during the loan term. Instead, they use one of two prepayment mechanisms designed to protect bond investors from reinvestment risk. Understanding these structures is essential for borrowers planning a potential sale or refinance before maturity.

Defeasance is the more common prepayment structure in CMBS. The borrower purchases a portfolio of U.S. Treasury securities that replicate the remaining loan payments (principal and interest), effectively substituting government bonds for the real estate collateral. The loan remains outstanding on paper, the bonds make all remaining payments, and the borrower is released from the mortgage. Defeasance costs depend on the relationship between the loan rate and current Treasury yields — in a rising rate environment, defeasance can actually produce a premium (profit) for the borrower.

Yield maintenance requires the borrower to make a lump-sum payment equal to the present value of the remaining interest payments the lender would have received, discounted at the applicable Treasury rate. Yield maintenance is more common in shorter-term CMBS loans and is generally simpler to calculate, though it can be more expensive than defeasance when rates have risen.

Most CMBS loans also include an open prepayment window during the final 3–6 months of the loan term, allowing the borrower to refinance or pay off the loan without penalty as maturity approaches. An experienced broker can help structure the loan term and prepayment provisions to align with the borrower's business plan.

What to Watch For

Common CMBS Pitfalls & How to Avoid Them

CMBS financing is powerful but comes with structural complexities that can catch unprepared borrowers off guard. Working with an experienced broker who understands CMBS documentation, servicing, and securitization mechanics can save borrowers significant time, money, and frustration.

Common Pitfalls

  • Rate lock timing: CMBS rates float until the loan is locked, typically 30–45 days before closing. Rate movements during underwriting can materially impact proceeds.
  • Reserve requirements: CMBS loans require funded reserves for taxes, insurance, capital expenditures, and rollover costs that reduce net loan proceeds at closing.
  • Special servicer transfers: If the loan enters distress, it transfers to a special servicer whose interests may not align with the borrower's.
  • Prepayment costs: Defeasance and yield maintenance can be expensive, particularly in a declining rate environment.
