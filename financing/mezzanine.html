Overview

Mezzanine Debt & Preferred Equity Financing

Mezzanine debt is secured by a pledge of ownership interest in the borrowing entity rather than a mortgage lien on the property, while preferred equity involves an actual equity investment with a preferred return. Both serve a similar function — increasing leverage and reducing sponsor equity requirements. These structures are commonly used for value-add acquisitions, development projects, and recapitalizations where senior lenders cap at 65-75% LTV.

Mezzanine & Preferred Equity FAQ

Mezzanine financing is subordinate debt that sits between senior mortgage debt and equity in the capital stack. Secured by a pledge of ownership interest rather than a property lien, mezzanine debt allows borrowers to increase total leverage to 80-90% of value while reducing their equity requirement.
Mezzanine debt is a loan secured by a pledge of the borrower's ownership interest, with fixed payments and foreclosure rights. Preferred equity is an actual equity investment with a preferred return, participating in ownership rather than lending. Both fill the gap between senior debt and common equity.
Mezzanine loan rates typically range from 10% to 18%, reflecting the higher risk position behind senior debt. Rates depend on the total leverage, property type, sponsor strength, and market conditions. Preferred equity returns are similar but may include additional upside participation.
Consider mezzanine financing when you need higher leverage than senior lenders will provide alone, want to reduce your equity requirement for an acquisition or development, are executing a value-add or development strategy, or need to recapitalize and pull equity from an existing property.
