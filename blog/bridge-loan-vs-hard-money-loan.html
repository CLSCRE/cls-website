Bridge Loans vs. Hard Money Loans: What's the Difference?

By Trevor Damyan March 13, 2026

When conventional financing timelines don't align with your commercial real estate opportunity, two alternative lending solutions frequently emerge in conversations with brokers and lenders: bridge loans and hard money loans. Both offer speed and flexibility that traditional bank financing cannot match, yet they serve distinctly different purposes and come with varying cost structures. Understanding these differences is critical for making informed financing decisions that align with your investment strategy and risk tolerance.

At CLS CRE, we've structured hundreds of bridge and hard money transactions across Los Angeles and throughout California. We've seen borrowers waste valuable time pursuing the wrong product, or worse, overpaying because they didn't understand the fundamental distinctions between these two financing vehicles. This comprehensive guide clarifies exactly when each loan type makes sense, what you'll actually pay, and how to determine which option serves your specific commercial real estate needs.

Understanding Bridge Loans: The Temporary Financing Solution

A commercial bridge loan is short-term financing designed to "bridge" the gap between an immediate capital need and a future liquidity event. These loans typically serve borrowers with strong exit strategies who need temporary financing while waiting for permanent debt placement, property stabilization, or sale proceeds from another asset.

Bridge loans are generally offered by institutional lenders, private debt funds, and specialized bridge lending divisions within larger financial organizations. The institutional nature of most bridge lenders means they maintain more standardized underwriting criteria than hard money lenders, though they remain significantly more flexible than conventional banks.

Typical Bridge Loan Terms and Pricing

As of March 2026, commercial bridge loan rates in the Los Angeles market typically range from 8.5% to 12.5%, depending on loan-to-value ratio, property type, and borrower experience. These rates reflect current market conditions and remain notably lower than hard money alternatives.

  • Interest Rates: 8.5-12.5% annually
  • Loan Terms: 12 to 36 months, with extension options
  • Loan-to-Value (LTV): Up to 75-80% of current property value
  • Loan-to-Cost (LTC): Up to 85-90% for value-add acquisitions
  • Closing Timeline: 15 to 30 days
  • Origination Fees: 1-3 points (1-3% of loan amount)

For example, a $3 million bridge loan at 10% annual interest on a stabilized multifamily property would cost approximately $25,000 monthly in interest-only payments. With a 2-point origination fee, you'd pay $60,000 upfront, for total first-year costs of $360,000 including fees and interest.

Common Bridge Loan Use Cases

Bridge loans excel in situations where borrowers need temporary financing with a clear path to refinancing or exit:

  • Property Stabilization: Purchasing an underperforming apartment building that needs 6-12 months of operational improvements before qualifying for agency debt
  • Cash-Out Refinancing Bridge: Extracting equity from one property to fund the down payment on another acquisition while permanent financing is arranged
  • Lease-Up Scenarios: Financing a commercial building with temporary vacancy while securing new tenants to meet permanent lender occupancy requirements
  • Value-Add Renovations: Acquiring and repositioning retail centers, office buildings, or industrial properties before refinancing into long-term debt
  • 1031 Exchange Timing: Securing replacement property quickly to meet exchange deadlines when seller financing or conventional loans move too slowly

Understanding Hard Money Loans: Asset-Based Lending

Hard money loans represent the most aggressive form of commercial real estate financing, with underwriting focused almost exclusively on the collateral property's value rather than borrower financials or creditworthiness. These loans come from private individuals or small lending companies willing to accept higher risk in exchange for premium returns.

The term "hard money" refers to the asset-backed nature of these loans—the "hard" collateral secures the debt, and lenders will foreclose and liquidate properties without hesitation if borrowers default. This collateral-centric approach enables hard money lenders to approve loans that institutional bridge lenders would decline.

Typical Hard Money Loan Terms and Pricing

Hard money loans carry significantly higher costs than bridge financing, reflecting their position as truly last-resort capital or ultra-fast execution tools:

  • Interest Rates: 11% to 18% annually (sometimes higher for distressed situations)
  • Loan Terms: 6 to 24 months
  • Loan-to-Value (LTV): Typically 50-70% maximum
  • Closing Timeline: 5 to 14 days (sometimes as fast as 72 hours)
  • Origination Fees: 2-5 points or more
  • Prepayment Penalties: Often 6-12 months of interest or substantial penalty fees

That same $3 million loan as hard money at 14% interest would cost $35,000 monthly in interest-only payments. With a 4-point origination fee ($120,000), your first-year total reaches $540,000—a $180,000 premium over comparable bridge financing.

When Hard Money Makes Sense

Despite higher costs, hard money loans serve critical functions in specific scenarios:

  • Extreme Speed Requirements: Competitive acquisition situations where closing within one week provides significant negotiating leverage or wins the deal
  • Credit or Income Challenges: Borrowers with recent bankruptcies, foreclosures, or complex income documentation who cannot qualify for institutional bridge loans
  • Distressed Property Acquisitions: Properties in severe disrepair or with code violations that institutional lenders won't touch
  • Heavy Lift Renovations: Ground-up construction or complete property transformations requiring lenders comfortable with significant execution risk
  • Creative Deal Structures: Transactions with unusual collateral, cross-collateralization needs, or non-standard terms that institutional underwriting cannot accommodate

Bridge Loan vs Hard Money: Direct Comparison

Understanding the key differences between these loan products helps clarify which option aligns with your situation:

Lender Profile and Sophistication

Bridge lenders typically operate as institutional entities with established underwriting departments, standardized documentation, and multiple layers of approval. These organizations fund loans from pooled investor capital or warehouse lines with major banks. Hard money lenders range from high-net-worth individuals funding loans personally to small companies with limited capital pools, resulting in more varied practices and requirements.

Underwriting Focus

A commercial bridge loan underwriting process examines property value, borrower experience, exit strategy feasibility, and financial capacity—though with more flexibility than conventional loans. Expect to provide personal financial statements, tax returns, rent rolls, operating statements, and detailed business plans. Bridge lenders want confidence you can execute your strategy and refinance or sell as planned.

Hard money underwriting fixates primarily on collateral value and equity cushion. Many hard money lenders care little about your credit score, income documentation, or business plan sophistication. If the property value supports the loan amount with adequate protective equity (typically 30-50% below the loan amount), they'll fund the deal. This collateral-only focus enables approvals that bridge lenders reject but comes at a significant cost premium.

Loan-to-Value Ratios

Bridge lenders commonly advance 75-80% of stabilized property value or 85-90% of total project cost for value-add deals. These higher LTV ratios mean less equity required from borrowers, making deals more accessible and preserving capital for renovations or other investments.

Hard money lenders typically cap LTV at 50-70%, requiring substantially more borrower equity. This conservative approach protects hard money lenders from loss if they must foreclose and liquidate the property quickly. For a $5 million property acquisition, a bridge loan might require $1 million down (80% LTV), while hard money could demand $1.5-2.5 million equity (50-70% LTV).

Closing Speed Differences

Bridge loans generally close in 15-30 days, offering significant speed advantages over conventional financing (45-90 days) while maintaining institutional documentation standards. This timeline accommodates thorough due diligence, appraisals, environmental reports, and legal review without the extensive committee approvals traditional banks require.

Hard money loans can close in 5-14 days, with some lenders capable of 72-hour funding in extreme circumstances. This speed advantage comes from minimal underwriting, abbreviated documentation, and decision-making concentrated in one or two individuals. When a once-in-a-decade acquisition opportunity requires seven-day closing to beat competing offers, hard money's speed justifies the cost premium.

Documentation and Complexity

Bridge loan documentation resembles conventional financing with detailed loan agreements, personal guarantees, environmental indemnities, and extensive property-level reporting requirements. Expect 100+ pages of closing documents and ongoing quarterly or annual financial reporting during the loan term.

Hard money documentation tends toward simplicity—a promissory note, deed of trust, and personal guarantee might constitute the entire package. Some hard money lenders operate with 10-20 pages of total documentation. This simplicity accelerates closing but often includes aggressive default provisions and foreclosure rights that favor lenders.

Cost Analysis: Running the Numbers

The true cost difference between bridge and hard money extends beyond stated interest rates. Let's examine a comprehensive example using realistic March 2026 market conditions in Los Angeles:

Scenario: $4 Million Multifamily Acquisition

Bridge Loan Terms:

  • Loan Amount: $3.2 million (80% LTV)
  • Interest Rate: 9.5%
  • Term: 24 months
  • Origination Fee: 2 points ($64,000)
  • Monthly Interest Payment: $25,333
  • Total Interest (24 months): $608,000
  • All-in Cost: $672,000

Hard Money Terms:

  • Loan Amount: $2.4 million (60% LTV)
  • Interest Rate: 14%
  • Term: 12 months
  • Origination Fee: 4 points ($96,000)
  • Monthly Interest Payment: $28,000
  • Total Interest (12 months): $336,000
  • All-in Cost: $432,000

At first glance, hard money appears cheaper over 12 months ($432,000 vs. $672,000 for 24 months of bridge financing). However, this comparison misleads because:

  • Bridge financing requires $800,000 equity while hard money demands $1.6 million—double the capital commitment
  • The $800,000 equity differential could earn returns elsewhere, representing significant opportunity cost
  • Bridge loan provides 24 months to execute renovations and stabilization versus 12 months with hard money, reducing execution pressure and refinancing risk
  • Refinancing from hard money to permanent debt after 12 months incurs additional transaction costs ($50,000-$100,000) while bridge allows more patient permanent debt timing

When accounting for equity efficiency and opportunity cost, bridge financing often delivers superior economics despite higher absolute interest costs over longer terms.

Evaluating Your Financing Needs: Decision Framework

Choosing between bridge and hard money requires honest assessment of your situation across several dimensions:

Choose Bridge Financing When:

  • You have reasonably strong credit (typically 650+ scores) and can document income or liquidity
  • Your timeline allows 20-30 days for closing without jeopardizing the opportunity
  • You're executing a value-add strategy requiring 12-24 months before refinancing makes sense
  • Maximizing leverage and minimizing equity requirements is important for portfolio management
  • The property is performing reasonably well or the distress is operational rather than physical
  • You want to preserve the option of extending the loan term if your exit strategy requires more time

Choose Hard Money When:

  • Closing speed is absolutely critical—you need 7-10 days maximum to secure the property
  • Your credit, income, or financial complexity makes institutional bridge approval unlikely
  • The property condition or situation is so distressed that bridge lenders won't consider it
  • You have significant liquid capital and the higher equity requirement isn't constraining
  • Your exit strategy is truly short-term (6-9 months) with high confidence in execution
  • You need maximum flexibility in loan structure or have unusual collateral situations

Questions to Ask Yourself

Before committing to either financing path, consider these critical questions:

What happens if my exit takes longer than planned? Bridge loans typically offer extension options (often with fees), while hard money lenders may force refinancing or sale through aggressive default provisions. Evaluate whether your business plan can tolerate the inflexibility of hard money if renovations run long or the refinancing market tightens.

Can I actually afford the monthly debt service? Use our DSCR calculator to determine whether property cash flow covers the debt payments. Hard money's higher rates might push monthly payments above rental income, requiring cash reserves to cover negative carry periods.

What's my backup plan if permanent refinancing doesn't materialize? Bridge lenders often maintain relationships with permanent debt sources and can facilitate transitions. Hard money lenders typically don't provide this support, leaving you to independently source refinancing under time pressure.

How does this financing affect my overall portfolio? High-cost hard money on one property might constrain your ability to secure financing for other opportunities. Bridge loans, with their more moderate costs, preserve borrowing capacity and optionality.

Hybrid Options and Alternative Structures

The bridge versus hard money decision isn't always binary. Sophisticated borrowers and brokers often structure hybrid approaches:

Bridge-to-Perm Programs

Some bridge lenders offer transition programs where the initial bridge loan converts to longer-term debt once the property stabilizes. These structures provide the flexibility of bridge financing with a pre-negotiated exit to permanent financing, eliminating refinancing risk and transaction costs.

Mezzanine Financing Alongside Lower Leverage

Combining a 60-65% LTV bridge loan with mezzanine debt (subordinate financing) can achieve higher total leverage without resorting to hard money. While mezzanine financing is expensive (12-16% rates), applying it only to the gap between senior bridge debt and your leverage target can be more cost-effective than hard money on the entire amount.

Short-Term Bridge with Hard Money Backup

For deals requiring fast closing but with strong fundamentals, you might close with hard money for speed, then immediately refinance into bridge or conventional debt
