Commercial Loan Rates Today (March 2026) — What Borrowers Need to Know

By Trevor Damyan March 15, 2026

If you are looking for commercial loan rates today, you are not alone. Rate inquiries are the single most common question we receive at CLS CRE, and for good reason: the difference between a well-priced and poorly-priced commercial mortgage can mean tens of thousands of dollars annually in interest expense. This guide provides a comprehensive, up-to-the-minute look at where commercial real estate interest rates stand in March 2026, what is driving them, and practical strategies for securing the best rate on your next deal.

Commercial Loan Rates Today: March 2026 Snapshot

Here is where commercial real estate loan rates stand as of mid-March 2026, based on pricing across our network of more than 1,000 lender relationships:

  • Permanent Loans (Stabilized Assets): 5.50% - 7.00% fixed, depending on property type, leverage, and sponsor strength
  • Agency Multifamily (Fannie Mae / Freddie Mac): 5.25% - 6.00% for stabilized properties with 1.25x+ DSCR
  • Life Company Loans: 5.50% - 6.25% for low-leverage, institutional-quality assets
  • CMBS Loans: 6.00% - 7.25% for conduit execution on stabilized commercial properties
  • Bridge Loans: 8.00% - 11.00% (SOFR + 300-550 bps), interest-only
  • Construction Loans: 7.00% - 10.00% (SOFR + 275-500 bps)
  • SBA 504 Loans: 5.00% - 6.50% effective blended rate for owner-occupied properties
  • DSCR Loans: 6.50% - 9.00%, depending on leverage and debt service coverage
  • Hard Money Loans: 10.00% - 14.00% with 2-5 points origination

These ranges represent realistic market pricing for borrowers with reasonably strong profiles. Weaker credits, secondary markets, or higher-risk property types will see rates at the upper end or above these ranges.

The Macroeconomic Forces Behind Today's Rates

Commercial loan rates do not exist in isolation. They are the product of several interconnected macroeconomic forces, and understanding these drivers helps borrowers anticipate where rates are heading and time their financing decisions accordingly.

The 10-Year Treasury Yield

The 10-Year U.S. Treasury yield is the single most important benchmark for fixed-rate commercial mortgages. As of mid-March 2026, the 10-Year is trading between 4.00% and 4.35%. This represents meaningful stability compared to the volatile 4.50%-5.00% range that characterized much of 2023 and 2024.

Permanent commercial mortgage rates are typically priced as a spread over the 10-Year Treasury, with spreads ranging from 150 to 300+ basis points depending on the lender type, property class, and leverage. When the 10-Year Treasury drops 25 basis points, fixed-rate commercial mortgages generally follow by a similar amount, though the pass-through is not always immediate or complete.

SOFR and Floating-Rate Loans

For floating-rate products like bridge loans, construction loans, and many DSCR loans, the Secured Overnight Financing Rate (SOFR) serves as the base index. SOFR currently sits at approximately 3.80%, reflecting the Federal Reserve's policy rate after several cuts from the 5.50% peak in mid-2023.

Bridge lenders price their loans as SOFR plus a margin (spread) that typically ranges from 300 to 550 basis points. A bridge loan priced at SOFR + 400 bps translates to roughly 7.80% today. As SOFR tracks closely with the Fed Funds rate, any additional Fed rate cuts would directly benefit floating-rate borrowers.

Federal Reserve Policy

The Federal Reserve has maintained a measured, data-dependent approach throughout early 2026. After cutting rates three times in late 2025, the Fed has paused to assess economic conditions. Core inflation has moderated to approximately 2.2%, close to the 2% target, which keeps the door open for one to two additional quarter-point cuts later this year.

For commercial real estate borrowers, this means the overall direction of rates is stable to slightly declining. However, any unexpected inflation uptick or geopolitical disruption could reverse this trajectory.

Lender Competition and Capital Availability

One of the most significant developments in 2026 has been the return of lender competition. Regional banks that pulled back from CRE lending during the rate volatility of 2023-2024 have selectively re-entered the market. Debt funds have raised substantial new capital and are actively deploying it. Life insurance companies have increased their commercial mortgage allocations.

This increased competition is compressing spreads, particularly for multifamily and industrial assets. For borrowers, more lenders competing for deals means better pricing and more favorable terms. Working with a broker who can access this full spectrum of capital sources is more valuable than ever.

Commercial Loan Rates by Property Type

Property type is one of the most significant determinants of your commercial loan rate. Lenders assign different risk premiums to different asset classes based on historical performance, current market fundamentals, and forward-looking demand projections.

Multifamily

Multifamily remains the most competitively priced asset class in commercial real estate. The combination of agency lending (Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac) and strong institutional demand creates deep liquidity that benefits borrowers.

  • Agency permanent (stabilized): 5.25% - 6.00%
  • Bank/life company permanent: 5.50% - 6.50%
  • Bridge (value-add): 8.00% - 9.50%
  • Construction: 7.25% - 9.50%

Multifamily cap rates have stabilized in the 4.5%-6.0% range nationally, and positive rent growth in most markets supports solid fundamentals for lenders.

Industrial and Warehouse

Industrial properties are a close second to multifamily in lender preference. E-commerce demand, supply chain reshoring, and limited new supply have maintained strong fundamentals.

  • Permanent: 5.50% - 6.50%
  • Bridge: 8.00% - 10.00%
  • Construction: 7.50% - 9.75%

Retail

The retail financing market has bifurcated sharply. Grocery-anchored and necessity-based centers finance well, while experiential and unanchored strip centers carry wider spreads.

  • Grocery-anchored permanent: 6.00% - 7.00%
  • Net lease (investment grade tenant): 5.75% - 6.50%
  • Unanchored strip: 7.00% - 8.50%

Office

Office remains the most challenged sector. Remote work continues to impact occupancy, and lenders are highly selective. Class A buildings in strong markets fare significantly better than suburban commodity product.

  • Class A (primary market): 6.50% - 7.50%
  • Class B/C or suburban: 7.50% - 9.00%+ (limited availability)
  • Bridge for repositioning: 10.00% - 13.00%

Hospitality

Hotels are seeing improving lender appetite as RevPAR recovery continues. Flagged properties with proven performance history attract the most competitive pricing.

  • Flagged permanent: 7.00% - 8.50%
  • Bridge (repositioning): 9.50% - 12.00%

Key Factors That Affect Your Specific Rate

While the ranges above provide useful benchmarks, your actual commercial loan rate depends on a combination of deal-specific and borrower-specific factors. Understanding these variables helps you position your loan request for optimal pricing.

Loan-to-Value Ratio (LTV)

Lower leverage means lower rates. A loan at 55% LTV will price 50-100 basis points tighter than the same loan at 75% LTV. Lenders reward conservative leverage with better pricing because their exposure to loss is reduced. If you can bring more equity to the table, you will be rewarded with a lower rate.

Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR)

DSCR measures the property's net operating income relative to its annual debt service obligation. Most lenders require a minimum DSCR of 1.20x-1.25x, meaning the property generates 20-25% more income than needed to cover the loan payment. Properties with DSCR above 1.40x often qualify for rate discounts. Use our DSCR calculator to evaluate your property's coverage ratio.

Borrower Credit and Experience

Your personal credit score, net worth, liquidity, and track record with similar properties all influence pricing. Experienced operators with strong financials and a portfolio of successfully managed properties will consistently receive better rate quotes than first-time buyers or borrowers with credit challenges.

Loan Size

Larger loans generally attract better pricing due to economies of scale for lenders. A $10 million loan will typically price 25-50 basis points tighter than a $1 million loan on otherwise identical terms. The sweet spot for most institutional lenders begins at $2-5 million.

Property Location

Primary markets (Los Angeles, New York, Miami, Dallas) typically command rates 25-50 basis points lower than secondary markets, which in turn price 25-50 basis points below tertiary markets. This spread reflects liquidity, lender familiarity, and exit certainty.

Loan Term and Structure

Shorter fixed-rate terms (5 years) generally price 25-75 basis points lower than longer terms (10 years) due to reduced interest rate risk for the lender. Interest-only periods, flexible prepayment provisions, and future advance features may add basis points to the rate but can deliver significant overall value.

How to Get the Best Commercial Loan Rate

The spread between the best and worst rate quotes for the same deal can be 150-250+ basis points. On a $5 million loan, that difference represents $75,000-$125,000 per year in interest expense. Here is how to position yourself for the best pricing:

1. Shop the Full Capital Market

Different lenders have different appetites at any given time. A regional bank that is aggressive on multifamily may not be competitive on retail. A life insurance company that dominates low-leverage industrial may not touch hospitality. A CMBS conduit may offer the best rate for a $7 million suburban office deal. Working with a broker who accesses all capital sources ensures you see every competitive option.

2. Present Institutional-Quality Loan Packages

Lenders price uncertainty. A clean, well-organized loan submission with detailed financials, current rent rolls, professional property photos, operating history, and a clear business plan reduces perceived risk. Sloppy or incomplete submissions signal execution risk, and lenders respond with wider spreads or outright declines.

3. Optimize Your DSCR and LTV

Before applying, run the numbers. Can you bring slightly more equity to drop LTV below a key threshold (such as 70% or 65%)? Can you increase NOI by addressing deferred maintenance, raising below-market rents, or reducing operating expenses? Small improvements to these metrics can yield meaningful rate reductions.

4. Consider Rate Locks Strategically

If your closing timeline extends beyond 45 days, or if you believe rates may rise, locking your rate at application protects against adverse movements. Many lenders offer 45-60 day locks at no cost, with extensions available for a modest fee (typically 12.5-25 basis points per 15-day extension). In the current stable environment, a rate lock provides certainty for your underwriting.

5. Optimize the Full Structure, Not Just Rate

A loan that is 25 basis points higher but includes a two-year interest-only period, flexible prepayment (step-down vs. yield maintenance), or a built-in future advance provision may deliver more total value than the absolute lowest rate. Think about total cost of capital over the life of the loan, not just the coupon rate.

When to Lock vs. When to Float

One of the most common questions we receive is whether to choose a fixed-rate or floating-rate loan. The answer depends on your business plan and risk tolerance.

Choose Fixed-Rate When:

  • You are acquiring a stabilized, income-producing property with a long-term hold horizon (5+ years)
  • You want payment certainty for budgeting and underwriting
  • You believe rates are at or near a cycle low and want to lock in current pricing
  • The property has stable, predictable cash flows that support a fully amortizing loan

Choose Floating-Rate When:

  • You have a short-term hold or business plan (1-3 years) such as value-add, lease-up, or repositioning
  • You want the flexibility to prepay without penalty
  • You believe the Fed will continue cutting rates, making your borrowing cost decline over time
  • You are executing a bridge-to-permanent strategy and want to refinance once the property stabilizes

In the current environment, with SOFR at 3.80% and potential additional cuts ahead, floating-rate borrowers may benefit from declining costs over the next 12-18 months. However, fixed rates offer valuable certainty that many investors prefer, especially for core holdings.

Rate Forecast: Where Are Commercial Loan Rates Heading?

The consensus view among capital markets professionals is that commercial loan rates will remain relatively stable through mid-2026, with modest downward pressure in the second half of the year if the Fed delivers one or two additional cuts.

Key forecasts:

  • 10-Year Treasury: Expected to trade in the 3.75%-4.50% range for the remainder of 2026
  • Permanent commercial mortgages: Could compress to 5.25%-6.75% by Q4 2026
  • Bridge loans: Likely to decline modestly as SOFR tracks Fed cuts lower
  • Spreads: Expected to remain compressed as lender competition intensifies

Importantly, we are unlikely to return to the sub-4% permanent rates of the pre-2022 era in the near term. Borrowers should not wait indefinitely for dramatically lower rates. The current environment represents a strong window of opportunity compared to 2023-2024 pricing.
