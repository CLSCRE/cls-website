Best Commercial Loan Lenders in 2026: Banks, Credit Unions, Private Lenders Compared

By Trevor Damyan March 15, 2026

Finding the right lender for your commercial real estate deal is arguably the most consequential financing decision you will make. The commercial lending landscape is vast and fragmented, with thousands of potential capital sources ranging from your local community bank to Wall Street conduits to Silicon Valley debt funds. Each lender type offers different rates, terms, leverage, speed, and flexibility. Choosing the wrong lender can cost you tens of thousands of dollars in excess interest, months of unnecessary delays, or a flat-out denial on a deal that another lender would happily fund.

This guide provides a comprehensive comparison of every major commercial real estate lender type operating in 2026, with current rate ranges, pros and cons, ideal use cases, and practical guidance for matching the right capital source to your specific deal.

1. Conventional Banks (Regional and National)

Banks remain the most common source of commercial real estate financing for deals between $500,000 and $10,000,000. They lend from their own balance sheets (portfolio loans) and typically maintain the loan for its full term rather than selling it on the secondary market.

Current Rates (March 2026)

  • 5-year fixed: 5.75% - 6.75%
  • 7-year fixed: 6.00% - 7.00%
  • 10-year fixed: 6.25% - 7.25%
  • Floating rate: SOFR + 200-350 bps (5.80% - 7.30%)

Typical Terms

  • LTV: 70-80%
  • DSCR minimum: 1.20x - 1.30x
  • Amortization: 20-25 years
  • Recourse: Full personal guarantee required
  • Closing timeline: 45-90 days
  • Minimum loan: $250,000 - $1,000,000 (varies by bank)

Pros

  • Relationship-based lending means flexibility on borderline deals
  • Wide range of property types accepted
  • Can bundle deposits, lines of credit, and other banking services
  • Local banks understand local markets deeply
  • Prepayment flexibility often better than CMBS or agency

Cons

  • Full recourse (personal guarantee) required on almost all loans
  • Rates typically 25-75 bps higher than life companies or agency for comparable deals
  • Subject to bank regulatory constraints that can tighten CRE lending appetite
  • Balloon maturity risk (5-10 year terms with 25-year amortization)
  • Can be slow and bureaucratic for larger loans requiring credit committee approval

Best For

Small to mid-size deals ($500K-$10M), borrowers who value a direct lending relationship, deals requiring flexibility on terms or structure, and borrowers with existing bank relationships who can leverage cross-sell opportunities for better pricing.

2. Credit Unions

Credit unions have emerged as increasingly competitive commercial lenders, particularly for smaller deals and owner-occupied properties. Their nonprofit structure allows them to offer rates and fees that often undercut traditional banks.

Current Rates (March 2026)

  • 5-year fixed: 5.50% - 6.50%
  • 7-10 year fixed: 5.75% - 6.75%

Typical Terms

  • LTV: 70-80%
  • DSCR minimum: 1.20x - 1.25x
  • Amortization: 20-30 years
  • Recourse: Usually full recourse
  • Closing timeline: 45-75 days
  • Maximum loan: Often capped at $5-15 million due to member lending limits

Pros

  • Often 25-50 bps lower than comparable bank rates
  • Lower fees (no profit motive)
  • More flexible underwriting for members in good standing
  • Community-oriented, often supportive of local business owners

Cons

  • Must be a member (usually easy to join)
  • Smaller loan capacity than banks
  • May lack expertise with complex or larger commercial deals
  • Limited geographic reach for some credit unions

Best For

Owner-occupied properties, smaller deals under $5M, borrowers who qualify for membership, and straightforward deals where the rate advantage outweighs any limitations on expertise or loan size.

3. Life Insurance Companies

Life insurance companies are among the most sophisticated and competitive commercial real estate lenders. They deploy policyholder reserves into high-quality CRE loans, targeting the lowest-risk segment of the market. If your deal qualifies, life company execution typically delivers the best rates available.

Current Rates (March 2026)

  • 10-year fixed: 5.50% - 6.25%
  • 15-year fixed: 5.75% - 6.50%
  • 20-year fixed: 6.00% - 6.75%

Typical Terms

  • LTV: 55-65% (conservative leverage)
  • DSCR minimum: 1.30x - 1.50x
  • Amortization: 25-30 years
  • Recourse: Non-recourse (with standard bad-boy carve-outs)
  • Closing timeline: 60-120 days
  • Minimum loan: $3,000,000 - $10,000,000 (most active above $5M)

Pros

  • Lowest rates in the market for qualifying deals
  • Non-recourse lending protects personal assets
  • Long-term fixed rates (10-25 years) provide payment certainty
  • Full-term amortization available (no balloon risk)
  • Sophisticated underwriting team that understands complex assets

Cons

  • Low leverage (55-65% LTV) requires substantial borrower equity
  • High minimum loan amounts ($3-10M+)
  • Selective on property quality (Class A or strong Class B only)
  • Slow closing process (60-120 days)
  • Strict prepayment penalties (yield maintenance or defeasance)
  • Limited appetite for retail, office, and hospitality in 2026

Best For

Large, stabilized, institutional-quality properties where the borrower has significant equity. Ideal for long-term hold investors seeking the lowest rate and non-recourse protection on permanent loans above $5 million.

4. CMBS Conduit Lenders

CMBS (Commercial Mortgage-Backed Securities) lenders originate commercial mortgages that are pooled, securitized, and sold to bond investors. This capital markets execution allows CMBS lenders to offer non-recourse loans with higher leverage than life companies, though at somewhat higher rates.

Current Rates (March 2026)

  • 5-year fixed: 6.00% - 6.75%
  • 10-year fixed: 6.25% - 7.25%

Typical Terms

  • LTV: Up to 75%
  • DSCR minimum: 1.25x - 1.35x
  • Amortization: 25-30 years
  • Recourse: Non-recourse (with carve-outs)
  • Closing timeline: 60-90 days
  • Minimum loan: $2,000,000 - $5,000,000

Pros

  • Non-recourse lending
  • Higher leverage than life companies (up to 75% LTV)
  • Assumable loans (valuable for future property sales)
  • Less focus on borrower financials, more on property cash flow
  • Available for a wide range of commercial property types

Cons

  • Inflexible loan terms (securitized loans cannot be easily modified)
  • Strict prepayment penalties (defeasance or yield maintenance only)
  • Special servicer involvement if property performance declines
  • No relationship benefit; each loan evaluated independently
  • Rates typically 50-100 bps above life company execution

Best For

Borrowers who want non-recourse protection with moderate leverage, investors who may sell the property before loan maturity (assumable loan benefits the buyer), and deals where the borrower's personal financial situation is complex but the property cash flow is strong.

5. Agency Lenders (Fannie Mae / Freddie Mac)

Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac are government-sponsored enterprises that provide the most competitive financing available for multifamily properties (5+ units). They do not lend directly; instead, approved lender-servicers originate and service loans on their behalf.

Current Rates (March 2026)

  • 5-year fixed: 5.25% - 5.75%
  • 7-year fixed: 5.40% - 5.90%
  • 10-year fixed: 5.50% - 6.00%
  • Floating rate: SOFR + 150-250 bps

Typical Terms

  • LTV: Up to 80%
  • DSCR minimum: 1.20x - 1.25x
  • Amortization: 30 years
  • Recourse: Non-recourse (with standard carve-outs)
  • Closing timeline: 45-75 days
  • Minimum loan: $1,000,000 (Freddie SBL) to $5,000,000+ (Fannie DUS)

Pros

  • Lowest rates for multifamily in the market
  • Non-recourse with high leverage (up to 80% LTV)
  • 30-year amortization standard
  • Interest-only periods available
  • Supplemental loans for future equity extraction
  • Assumable loans

Cons

  • Multifamily only (5+ units); no commercial property types
  • Requires stabilized occupancy (typically 90%+ for 90+ days)
  • Net worth and liquidity requirements (net worth equal to loan, 10% liquid)
  • Cannot finance properties requiring significant renovation
  • Strict prepayment provisions

Best For

Any stabilized multifamily property with 5+ units. If you own apartments, agency financing should be your first consideration. The combination of low rates, high leverage, non-recourse terms, and 30-year amortization is unmatched by any other capital source.

6. SBA Lenders (504 and 7(a) Programs)

The Small Business Administration guarantees loans made by approved lenders, enabling favorable terms for small business owners purchasing or refinancing owner-occupied commercial real estate. SBA loans are among the most accessible financing options for first-time commercial property buyers.

Current Rates (March 2026)

  • SBA 504 (blended rate): 5.00% - 6.50%
  • SBA 7(a) variable: Prime + 1.00% - 2.75% (currently 7.50% - 9.25%)
  • SBA 7(a) fixed: 6.50% - 8.00%

Typical Terms

  • LTV: Up to 90% (504) or 85-90% (7(a))
  • DSCR minimum: 1.15x - 1.25x
  • Amortization: 20-25 years (504 real estate) or up to 25 years (7(a))
  • Recourse: Full personal guarantee
  • Closing timeline: 60-120 days
  • Maximum loan: $5,000,000 (504 CDC portion) or $5,000,000 (7(a))

Pros

  • Lowest down payment available (10% for 504)
  • Long-term fixed rates through 504 program
  • Available to borrowers with limited CRE experience
  • Government guarantee reduces lender risk, improving approval odds
  • Can finance equipment and working capital alongside real estate (7(a))

Cons

  • Owner-occupancy required (51%+ for most programs)
  • Slow approval and closing process (60-120 days)
  • Extensive documentation and paperwork
  • SBA guarantee fee adds to closing costs (0.5-3.5% of guaranteed portion)
  • 7(a) variable rates can be high and increase with Fed hikes
  • Full recourse with personal guarantee

Best For

Small business owners buying property for their own operations, first-time commercial buyers who need low down payments, and businesses that need to finance both real estate and equipment in a single transaction.

7. Private Debt Funds and Bridge Lenders

Bridge lenders and private debt funds provide short-term financing for transitional commercial properties that do not yet qualify for permanent financing. They have become a critical part of the CRE lending ecosystem, funding value-add acquisitions, lease-up strategies, and property repositioning.

Current Rates (March 2026)

  • Institutional bridge: 8.00% - 11.00% (SOFR + 300-550 bps)
  • Middle-market bridge: 9.00% - 12.00%
  • Hard money: 10.00% - 14.00%

Typical Terms

  • LTV: 65-80% of current value (up to 90% of cost)
  • DSCR minimum: 1.00x or below (focus on exit strategy)
  • Term: 12-36 months with extensions
  • Recourse: Varies (some non-recourse available above $5M)
  • Closing timeline: 15-45 days (hard money 5-14 days)
  • Minimum loan: $500,000 - $2,000,000

Pros

  • Fast closing (15-45 days, as fast as 5 days for hard money)
  • Flexible on property condition and occupancy
  • Will finance deals other lenders decline
  • Interest-only payments preserve cash flow during renovation
  • Creative structures (future advance, construction holdbacks)

Cons

  • Highest rates in the market
  • Origination fees of 1-5 points
  • Short terms create refinancing pressure
  • Exit strategy must be viable or default risk increases
  • Hard money lenders may have aggressive foreclosure practices

Best For

Value-add acquisitions requiring renovation, properties with temporary vacancy or below-market occupancy, time-sensitive acquisitions where speed is essential, and deals that need a bridge to permanent financing once the property is stabilized.

8. Mezzanine and Preferred Equity Providers

Mezzanine lenders and preferred equity providers fill the gap between senior debt and borrower equity, enabling higher total leverage on commercial deals. These are not standalone lenders but rather supplement your primary mortgage.

Current Rates (March 2026)

  • Mezzanine debt: 10.00% - 16.00%
  • Preferred equity: 12.00% - 18.00% (plus potential profit participation)

Typical Terms

  • Position: Subordinate to first mortgage, senior to common equity
  • Total leverage: 75-90% of value (combined with senior debt)
  • Term: Coterminous with senior debt or 2-5 years
  • Minimum: Typically $1,000,000+

Best For

Experienced investors who need to minimize equity in a deal, recapitalizations where the borrower wants to extract equity without a full refinance, and large deals where maximizing leverage improves return on equity.

How to Choose the Right Lender for Your Deal

With so many lender types available, choosing the right one requires matching your deal characteristics to the lender's sweet spot. Here is a practical decision framework:

Match by Property Type

  • Multifamily (5+ units, stabilized): Agency (Fannie/Freddie) first, then life companies or banks
  • Multifamily (value-add): Bridge lenders, then agency for takeout
  • Industrial/warehouse: Life companies for low leverage, banks for moderate leverage, CMBS for non-recourse
  • Retail (credit tenant): Banks, CMBS, or life companies
  • Office: Banks (selective), CMBS (for strong assets)
  • Owner-occupied: SBA 504 or 7(a), or conventional bank
  • Transitional/value-add: Bridge lenders or debt funds

Match by Loan Size

  • Under $1M: Local bank, credit union, SBA
  • $1M - $5M: Regional bank, credit union, SBA, Freddie SBL
  • $5M - $20M: Banks, CMBS, agency, life companies
  • $20M+: Life companies, CMBS, agency, institutional debt funds

Match by Priority

  • Lowest rate: Life company or agency
  • Highest leverage: SBA (90% LTV) or agency (80% LTV)
  • Non-recourse: CMBS, agency, or life company
  • Fastest closing: Bridge/hard money (5-30 days)
  • Most flexibility: Bank or credit union (portfolio loans)
  • Lowest down payment: SBA 504 (10% down)

The commercial lending market is fragmented by design. No single lender is the best option for every deal. A bank that aggressively prices multifamily may decline retail. Frequently Asked Questions

The main types of commercial real estate lenders include: conventional banks (regional and national), credit unions, life insurance companies, CMBS conduits, agency lenders (Fannie Mae/Freddie Mac for multifamily), SBA lenders, private debt funds, bridge lenders, and hard money lenders. Each has different rate ranges, leverage limits, property preferences, and approval requirements.
Life insurance companies typically offer the lowest rates (5.50%-6.25% in 2026) but require low leverage (55-65% LTV) and institutional-quality properties. Agency lenders (Fannie Mae/Freddie Mac) offer the next best rates (5.25%-6.00%) exclusively for multifamily. SBA 504 loans offer competitive blended rates (5.00%-6.50%) for owner-occupied properties. The "best" rate depends on your specific property, leverage needs, and borrower profile.
A commercial mortgage broker provides access to hundreds or thousands of lender relationships across all capital source types, saving you time and often securing better terms than you could negotiate directly. Brokers are most valuable when your deal is above $1 million, when you need to compare multiple capital sources, or when your deal has complexity that a single bank might not accommodate. For smaller, straightforward deals with an existing banking relationship, going direct can work well.
Match the lender type to your property type, business plan, and timeline. Use banks for relationship-based deals under $5M, life companies for low-leverage institutional properties, agency for multifamily, CMBS for larger non-recourse deals, SBA for owner-occupied, bridge lenders for transitional properties, and debt funds for flexible structures. A commercial mortgage broker can help identify the best match.
Recourse loans hold the borrower personally liable for the full loan amount if the property income is insufficient to cover payments. Non-recourse loans limit the lender's recovery to the property itself, protecting the borrower's personal assets (with standard carve-outs for fraud, misrepresentation, and environmental issues). CMBS, agency, and life company loans are typically non-recourse. Bank loans are usually recourse.
