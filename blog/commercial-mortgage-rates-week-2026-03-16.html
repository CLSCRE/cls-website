Commercial Mortgage Rates: Week of March 16, 2026

By Trevor Damyan March 16, 2026

Rate Summary: Relief Rally Brings Borrowing Costs Down

The week of March 16-20, 2026 delivered welcome news for commercial real estate borrowers as interest rates declined across the capital stack. The 10-year Treasury yield dropped 12 basis points to close Friday at 4.18%, while the 5-year Treasury fell 9 basis points to 3.94%. This move lower translated directly into commercial mortgage pricing, with most permanent loan programs seeing rate improvements of 10-15 basis points compared to last week's levels.

The catalyst for this week's rate relief came from dovish commentary by multiple Federal Reserve officials, including Vice Chair Philip Jefferson's Wednesday remarks suggesting the central bank would maintain its current policy stance through at least mid-year. With inflation data continuing to moderate—the most recent CPI print showed core inflation at 2.6% year-over-year—markets are increasingly confident that the Fed's tightening cycle has concluded.

For context, commercial mortgage rates remain elevated compared to the historic lows of 2020-2021, but we're now seeing the most favorable borrowing conditions since early 2024. The combination of stable Fed policy, improving Treasury yields, and normalized credit spreads has created a constructive environment for deal execution across property types.

Treasury Yields: Technical Factors and Economic Data

This week's Treasury rally was driven by a confluence of technical and fundamental factors. Beyond the Fed commentary, several data points reinforced the narrative of economic normalization rather than contraction:

  • Retail Sales: February retail sales came in at +0.3% month-over-month, slightly below the +0.5% consensus but showing continued consumer resilience
  • Housing Starts: February housing starts increased 6.2% to a seasonally adjusted annual rate of 1.52 million units, suggesting confidence in the residential sector
  • Industrial Production: A modest +0.2% gain indicated manufacturing stability without overheating concerns
  • Initial Jobless Claims: Claims held steady at 215,000, consistent with a healthy but cooling labor market

The yield curve dynamics also merit attention. The 2-year/10-year spread steepened to +34 basis points this week, the most positive slope we've seen since mid-2022. A steeper curve typically signals improved economic confidence and reduced recession fears, which supports risk assets including commercial real estate. For borrowers, this curve normalization is particularly beneficial because it reduces the pricing advantage of shorter-term debt, making 7-10 year fixed-rate loans more competitive relative to 5-year products.

Looking at credit spreads specifically, the 10-year swap spread held relatively tight at 42 basis points, while Agency CMBS spreads compressed slightly to T+145 for AAA-rated paper. Life company spreads have been remarkably stable in the T+165 to T+185 range depending on property type and leverage, reflecting the patient capital approach these lenders maintain.

What It Means for Borrowers: Timing and Strategy

This week's rate movement creates several strategic considerations for active borrowers and those contemplating refinances in the coming quarters:

Refinance Window Opening: Borrowers with loans maturing in Q2 or Q3 2026 should accelerate their refinance planning. The current rate environment is the most favorable we've seen in over two years, and while we don't anticipate a dramatic spike in rates, the path of least resistance from current levels is likely sideways to modestly higher rather than significantly lower.

Lock Timing: For transactions currently in process, this week presented good execution opportunities. We generally advise clients to lock rates when they're within 15-20 basis points of their target, rather than trying to time the absolute bottom. The 10-15 basis point improvement this week brought many programs into that target zone.

Property Type Considerations: Lender appetite remains differentiated by asset class. Multifamily, industrial, and self-storage continue to receive the most aggressive pricing and leverage. Office properties, particularly suburban Class B and C assets, face continued scrutiny with wider spreads and more conservative proceeds. Retail has bifurcated sharply between grocery-anchored/necessity retail (strong execution) and enclosed malls (limited options).

Bridge-to-Permanent Strategy: With stabilized rates and a normalized lending environment, the urgency to use bridge financing as a "rate-wait" strategy has diminished. For properties with clean rent rolls and occupancy above 85%, moving directly to permanent financing often makes more sense than incurring bridge loan costs while hoping for better future rates.

CRE Loan Rate Ranges by Product Type

Based on actual term sheets and commitments issued by CLS CRE this week, here are current rate ranges for various commercial mortgage products. Note that final pricing depends on property type, location, sponsorship, leverage, and other underwriting factors:

Agency Loans (Fannie Mae/Freddie Mac Multifamily):

  • 5-year fixed: 5.45% - 5.75%
  • 7-year fixed: 5.65% - 5.95%
  • 10-year fixed: 5.85% - 6.15%
  • Maximum leverage: 80% LTV on stabilized properties, 75% on lease-up
  • Minimum DSCR: 1.25x

Life Company Loans (All Property Types):

  • 10-year fixed: 6.05% - 6.45%
  • 15-year fixed: 6.25% - 6.65%
  • Maximum leverage: 65% LTV (multifamily/industrial), 60% LTV (office/retail)
  • Minimum DSCR: 1.30x
  • Note: Life companies offering the most competitive execution on $10M+ loans with institutional-quality properties

CMBS/Conduit Loans:

  • 5-year fixed: 6.15% - 6.55%
  • 7-year fixed: 6.35% - 6.75%
  • 10-year fixed: 6.45% - 6.85%
  • Maximum leverage: 75% LTV
  • Minimum DSCR: 1.25x
  • Sweet spot: $3M-$20M loan amounts on stabilized income-producing properties

Bank Portfolio Loans:

  • 5-year fixed: 6.25% - 6.85%
  • 5/1 ARM: 6.10% - 6.65%
  • 7-year fixed: 6.45% - 7.05%
  • Maximum leverage: 70% LTV (varies significantly by bank and relationship)
  • Minimum DSCR: 1.25x-1.35x
  • Note: Regional and community banks offering the most flexibility on smaller loans ($1M-$10M) and non-traditional properties

Bridge/Transitional Loans:

  • Floating rate (SOFR + spread): SOFR + 3.75% to SOFR + 5.50%
  • All-in rates currently: 8.60% - 10.35%
  • Maximum leverage: 75% LTC on value-add, 70% LTV on stabilized bridge
  • Terms: 12-36 months with extension options
  • Best execution: Properties requiring lease-up, renovation, or other business plan execution before qualifying for permanent financing

Market Outlook: Stability with Selective Opportunities

Looking forward, we expect the commercial mortgage rate environment to remain relatively stable through Q2 2026, with potential for modest improvement if economic data continues to support the soft-landing narrative. Several factors support this outlook:

Fed Policy Path: The futures market is pricing in one potential 25-basis-point rate cut by December 2026, down from expectations of two cuts just a month ago. This measured approach should keep volatility contained and allow commercial real estate fundamentals to drive lending decisions rather than rate uncertainty.

Credit Availability: Lending standards have normalized after the tightening of 2023-2024. Life companies, agencies, and CMBS lenders all have robust capital to deploy, creating healthy competition for quality deals. The days of aggressive underwriting and 85% leverage on marginal properties remain in the past, but well-sponsored deals on solid assets are finding excellent execution.

Property Market Fundamentals: The bifurcation in commercial real estate continues to widen. Multifamily markets are seeing rent growth moderate but occupancy remain healthy in supply-constrained markets. Industrial demand continues to benefit from supply chain reconfiguration and e-commerce growth. Office remains challenged, particularly in gateway cities with high downtown vacancy rates. Self-storage and specialty sectors like medical office and life sciences are finding strong lender support.

Potential Headwinds: Risks to monitor include any unexpected inflation resurgence (particularly in services), geopolitical developments that could affect Treasury yields, or deterioration in commercial real estate operating fundamentals. The office sector deserves particular attention, as continued work-from-home adoption could create broader market concerns even for borrowers in other property types.

Action Items for Borrowers

Based on current market conditions, we recommend the following actions for commercial real estate borrowers:

Immediate (Next 30 Days):

  • Borrowers with loans maturing in Q2-Q3 2026 should request refinance proposals now to lock in current pricing
  • Active transactions should consider rate locks if within 20 basis points of target rates
  • Acquisition financing seekers should finalize loan structures and submit applications to capture current rate levels

Near-Term (60-90 Days):

  • Evaluate 2027 maturity loans for early refinance opportunities, particularly if current loan has prepayment flexibility
  • Properties in lease-up or stabilization phase should create detailed refinance timelines with target leverage and rate parameters
  • Consider hybrid strategies like partial interest rate hedging for borrowers wanting to maintain some exposure to potential rate improvement

Long-Term Planning:

  • Update portfolio maturity schedules and create proactive refinance strategies
  • Evaluate property-level capital improvements that could enhance refinance proceeds and lower debt costs
  • Build relationships with multiple capital sources to ensure competitive execution when refinance windows open

The commercial mortgage market is offering the best execution environment in over two years. While rates remain above historic lows, the combination of stable Treasury yields, normalized credit spreads, and robust lender competition creates opportunities for borrowers to secure attractive long-term financing. As always, individual deal circumstances vary significantly, and working with an experienced commercial mortgage advisor helps navigate lender selection, rate lock timing, and structural optimization.

Based on current market conditions as of March 2026, borrowers should consider refinancing now rather than waiting for significantly lower rates. The Fed has signaled a stable policy stance through mid-year, and Treasury yields are unlikely to decline dramatically from current levels. If your loan is maturing within 6-12 months or you're within 15-20 basis points of your target rate, moving forward makes strategic sense. The risk of waiting—potential rate increases or deal complications closer to maturity—generally outweighs the uncertain benefit of trying to time the absolute rate bottom. That said, each situation is unique, and borrowers should evaluate their specific prepayment penalties, maturity timeline, and rate targets with their advisor.
Agency loans (Fannie Mae/Freddie Mac) and life company loans serve different borrower needs. Agency loans typically offer higher leverage (up to 80% LTV vs. 65% for life companies), lower debt service coverage requirements (1.25x vs. 1.30x), and slightly lower interest rates. They're ideal for experienced multifamily borrowers seeking maximum proceeds. Life company loans provide longer fixed-rate terms (15-30 years vs. typically 10 years maximum for agencies), more flexible prepayment options, and can accommodate mixed-use properties or assets that don't fit standard agency boxes. Life companies also typically have faster closing timelines and fewer third-party reports. The choice depends on your leverage needs, property type, desired loan term, and timing requirements.
Office property lending in March 2026 remains highly bifurcated by quality and location. Class A office in strong suburban markets with high occupancy (85%+) and long-term lease terms can still achieve competitive financing, though typically at 55-60% LTV versus the 65% that was standard pre-2020. Downtown office in major cities faces the most scrutiny, particularly properties with significant near-term lease rollover or occupancy below 80%. Lenders are focusing heavily on tenant credit quality, lease term remaining, and building efficiency/amenities that attract post-pandemic tenants. Medical office buildings and life sciences properties are receiving much more favorable treatment than traditional office. Borrowers with office properties should expect lower leverage, higher rates (typically 50-75 basis points above multifamily/industrial), and more extensive underwriting of tenant rollover risk and market position.
