Commercial Real Estate Market Report: Miami 2026

By Trevor Damyan October 21, 2025

Miami has transformed from a tourism-dependent economy into a global financial and technology hub, attracting corporate relocations, hedge fund establishments, and international capital at an unprecedented pace. The metro's combination of no state income tax, international connectivity, and quality of life has made it one of the most sought-after commercial real estate markets in the world, with pricing that increasingly reflects gateway-city fundamentals.

Miami Market Overview: Key Metrics

The Miami commercial real estate market in 2026 reflects a market shaped by finance, international trade, tourism, technology, healthcare. Here are the key metrics investors and borrowers should know:

  • Multifamily Vacancy: 5.2% — near the national average with healthy absorption
  • Industrial Vacancy: 3.5% — among the tightest markets nationally
  • Office Vacancy: 14.2%
  • Retail Vacancy: 4.1%
  • Rent Growth: 5.5% year-over-year
  • Job Growth: 2.5% — outpacing the national average
  • Population Growth: 1.2% annually
  • Median Asking Rent: $2,450

Multifamily Outlook in Miami

Miami multifamily commands some of the highest rents in the Sun Belt, with median asking rents at $2,450 and rent growth running at an impressive 5.5% year-over-year. Vacancy at 5.2% reflects strong demand from both domestic migration (primarily from the Northeast) and the city's growing young professional workforce in finance and technology. The luxury rental segment in Brickell and Edgewater has performed particularly well as high-income renters opt for flexibility over ownership.

Industrial & Logistics Market

Miami's industrial market is one of the tightest in the Southeast at 3.5% vacancy, driven by the metro's role as a gateway for Latin American trade, e-commerce distribution to South Florida's 6+ million consumers, and growing cold storage demand from the region's food service industry. Airport-adjacent logistics facilities and Doral warehouse space command premium rents with limited new supply available.

Office & Retail Dynamics

The Miami office market has outperformed most major metros, with financial services firms and technology companies driving absorption in Brickell and Coconut Grove. Vacancy at 14.2% is well below the national average. Retail fundamentals are exceptionally strong at 4.1% vacancy, supported by tourism spending, population growth, and the metro's outdoor lifestyle that drives foot traffic to open-air retail formats.

Financing Landscape in Miami

Miami attracts global capital, including significant cross-border lending from international banks and foreign debt funds alongside domestic lenders. The market's premium pricing and strong fundamentals support competitive financing across all property types. Agency multifamily lending is aggressive, while life companies and CMBS conduits actively pursue industrial and Class A office. Private wealth and family office capital provide an additional layer of debt and equity.

For borrowers in the Miami-Fort Lauderdale-Pompano Beach area, current commercial mortgage rates range from 4.75% for agency multifamily to higher rates for transitional and value-add projects. Key factors that influence your rate include property type, leverage, sponsor experience, and asset location within the metro.

Top Submarkets to Watch

The Miami metro features several distinct submarkets that present unique investment opportunities:

  • Brickell
  • Wynwood
  • Doral
  • Coral Gables
  • Fort Lauderdale
  • West Palm Beach

Each of these submarkets has distinct characteristics in terms of tenant demand, development activity, and pricing. The top investment corridors in Miami include Brickell financial district, Wynwood creative corridor, Doral industrial, Fort Lauderdale mixed-use.

Investment Outlook: Miami 2026

Miami's trajectory as a global financial center appears durable, with ongoing corporate relocations, infrastructure investment (including Brightline rail expansion), and international capital flows supporting continued appreciation. Insurance costs remain a key risk factor that all investors must underwrite carefully. The strongest opportunities in 2026 are workforce multifamily outside the urban core, airport-adjacent industrial, and mixed-use development along the Brightline corridor.
